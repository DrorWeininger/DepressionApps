Psychological Interventions Using Digital Technologies

Nermin Halilovic & Dror Weininger

 

 

Impact of Smartphone App–Based Psychological Interventions for Reducing Depressive Symptoms in People With Depression: Systematic Literature Review and Meta-analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials

by Serrano-Ripoll et al.

 

 

The Meta-Analysis In Overview

 

 

Introduction

This meta-analysis analyses the impact of app based psychological interventions on depressive symptoms in individuals with depression. Depression is already highly prevalent worldwide, and by 2030 it's expected to become the world's leading contributor to the global disease burden. It is characterized by a wide range of symptoms, which significantly impact an individual's functioning. Most of those who suffer from depression do not receive treatment, with barriers ranging from geographical and financial to societal. (Strawbridge et al., 2022) showed that among those who are diagnosed with a major depressive episode, around 38% do not receive any treatment.

Some major advantages of app-based interventions are the enhancement of patients’ autonomy, the lower cost compared to seeing a therapist, the prevention of relapse and the overall higher availability. Many apps claim to have a positive effect on mental health. However, this is unaccounted for by most of them. Most mental health apps are not tested in clinical settings, and some may even promote ineffective or harmful practices (Baumel et al., 2020). Therefore, scientific evaluation is crucial to determine whether app-based psychological interventions effectively reduce depressive symptoms.

Serrano-Ripoll et al. (2022) investigated specifically the effects of psychological interventions delivered through smartphone apps on depressive symptoms. Most of these interventions included either standalone Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) or CBT combined with cognitive control therapy, positive psychology, brief behavioral activation or mindfulness and acceptance-based therapy. To our and the author's knowledge, this is the first meta-analysis to focus exclusively on randomized controlled trials in which a smartphone application serves as the main therapeutic component for patients with depression.

 

 

What is this study about?

Serrano-Ripoll et al. analyzed the impact of app-based psychological interventions for reducing depressive symptoms in people with depression. To do this they summarized 12 studies that measured the effects of smartphone apps which were developed with the aim of reducing depression compared to a control group. The studies were published between the years 2015-2020 and contained data from a total sample of 2018 participants with depressive symptoms ranging from mild-to-moderate to moderate-to-severe. To be included in the meta-analysis, the studies were required to be empirical studies that examined the impact of smartphone app based interventions on depressive symptoms, using a random-controlled trials design. Participants in all studies were to be diagnosed with depressive symptoms using valid and reliable measures, and the smartphone apps used in the interventions were required to be either the only or a central component of the intervention. For the full screening and inclusion process, see figure 1. Two studies which were included in the systematic review were excluded from the meta-analysis due to the lack of data for the calculation of an effect size (Arean et al., 2016; Pratap et al., 2018). The two studies pursued more methodological questions about the feasibility of researching the effects of smartphone apps on depressive symptoms, and did not look into the effects of those per-se. Thus, the data presented in those studies wasn't suitable for goals of the current meta-analysis, although they provided some other interesting insights regarding compliance with the app protocol.

 

Figure 1. PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Item for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) flow diagram.

 

The studies were reviewed for their risk of bias according to the Cochrane risk of bias tool. 4 studies were considered at low risk of bias, 2 raised some concerns and 4 raised high concerns of bias. The most prominent areas of risk were deviation from the intended intervention (4 studies with high risk of bias), missing outcome data (2 high risk studies and 1 that raised some concerns) and randomization (3 studies raised some concerns of bias). The Begg and Egger tests were used to exclude publication bias in the meta-analysis.

 

Figure 2. Risk of bias assessment.

 

Due to the assumption that the included studies would differ in setting, methods and design, a random effects model was used to pool the data. Effect sizes were pooled based on the Cohen's d (referred to as SMD in the paper), with scaled reversed for some of the studies in order to reach a consistent scale (a higher score = higher level of depression). Additionally, heterogeneity was measured using the I2, with I2>50 considered substantial and an indicator that the sample was too heterogeneous to be attributed to the same effect.

A post-hoc exploratory regression was made to identify potential factors that moderate the effects of smartphone app interventions on depression. The factors that were assessed were depression severity (mild to moderate vs moderate to severe), therapeutic approaches (CBT, CBT + others and behavioral activation), intervention's duration (1-7 vs 8-12 weeks), comparator (usual care vs minimal intervention), components of the intervention (multifaceted vs single-component interventions), communication directionality (unidirectional vs bidirectional communication) and the methods used to assess depression (diagnostic instruments vs validated self-report measures).

 

 

What does this study find out?

The meta-analysis, which pooled the effects from 10 studies, found smartphone apps effective at decreasing depressive symptoms. This effect is moderate (SMD=-0.51; p<.001, I2=70%). Due to the high heterogeneity, a sensitivity analysis was performed, which excluded two studies that contributed the largest heterogeneity to the pool. This sensitivity analysis yielded a larger, yet still moderate effect (SMD=-0.61; p<.001, I2=34%).

A second sensitivity analysis was conducted excluding four studies which were found to raise a high risk of bias. For the second sensitivity analysis a moderate, yet smaller, significant effect has been observed (SMD −0.41; p=.009, I2=71.6%). As for the high level of heterogeneity in the pool used for the second sensitivity analysis, no further explanation was given. For all three analyses the Egger and Begg tests suggested an absence of publication bias.

 

Figure 3. Effect of apps on depressive symptoms compared with active treatment and control conditions.

 

As for the contribution of moderators to the effect of smartphone apps on depression, the exploratory post-hoc meta-regression analyses found only symptom severity to have a significant contribution. Patients with moderate to severe depression were found to gain better results from the use of the apps. The subgroup difference between them and participants with mild to moderate depression was found significant (β = −0.49; p=.003). All other moderator subgroup differences were found insignificant.

 

 

How do we assess this meta-analysis?

We assessed the meta-analysis according to the five MAGIC criteria, presented by Abelson (1995).

 

How substantial was the effect?

According to a random effects meta-analysis, the interventions had a statistically significant and moderate effect in reducing depressive symptoms compared with control conditions in which participants received usual care or minimal intervention (SMD=-0.51). A sensitivity analysis that excluded 2 trials with highest heterogeneity from the meta-analysis, yielded an even larger effect (SMD = −0.61), and even the smallest effect found, yielded by a sensitivity analysis that excluded 33% (4/12) of the trials with high risk of bias (SMD=-0.41), is comparable to the average effect size of antidepressants, which is also moderate      (d=~0.3; Khan et al., 2021). Therefore, we found this effect substantial.

 

How differentiated are the findings?

The effect presented in the meta-analysis was found moderate in comparison with usual care or minimal intervention. In addition, the post-hoc moderator analysis found a significant moderation of depression severity on the effectiveness of the apps (β = −0.49), with moderate to severely depressed patients benefiting more from the apps. All other 6 moderators such as intervention type, duration, components etc. were found insignificant, which doesn’t allow to draw conclusions regarding what makes a mental health app successful in reducing depressive symptoms. Thus, although the meta-analysis gives us some information about the conditions required for the success of an application-based intervention, there is much more that should be discovered before they can be optimally utilized and be adapted to the needs of patients.

 

How generalizable are the findings?

Another way to interpret the lack of moderators is as an evidence of the stability of the effects found in the meta-analysis. It seems that the effects of the different apps were robust across different variations, assessment methods of depression, psychotherapeutic methods etc. On the other hand, some limitations such as the lack of quality data, risks of bias in the studies and overall low compliance rates caused the authors to be reluctant to give significant recommendations for clinicians regarding the clinical use of apps as part of therapy, and more prone to advice for further research before more decisive conclusions can be drawn (Serrano-Ripoll et al., 2022). Like the authors, we also concluded that the present meta-analysis suggests that therapeutic apps are worthy of further research and development, but also that the current knowledge is not enough to draw conclusions about the general depressive population.

 

Why is this meta-analysis relevant?

This is the first meta-analysis to focus exclusively on randomized controlled trials in which a smartphone app serves as the main therapeutic component for patients with depression. As outlined in the introduction, depression is a leading and increasingly prevalent cause of disability and suicide worldwide. Many individuals face financial, geographical or societal barriers when seeking traditional therapy. Smartphone applications have the potential to overcome these barriers and offer low-cost, on-demand therapy that can be personalized to the patients' needs regarding location, duration and frequency.  The results of this analysis indicate that psychological interventions delivered through smartphone apps can be effective in the treatment of depression. These findings indicate the need for further development and evaluation of high-quality digital interventions. 

 

Are the methodology and claims found in this study reliable?

Due to the transparent research design and the rigorous procedure, this meta-analysis can be considered methodologically reliable. To identify relevant studies, six major databases were searched with the use of well-defined Medical Subjects Headings and free-text terms. To minimize selection bias, two reviewers independently screened the titles, abstracts and full texts for the study selection. The data extraction and the risk-of-bias assessments were also performed independently with standardized tools such as the Cochrane Risk of Bias and ROBINS-I. Publication bias was assessed with funnel plots and the Begg and Egger Test. Disagreements or discrepancies during those steps were resolved by reaching a consensus or involving a third reviewer. For statistical analysis, a random-effects model was used, and sensitivity analyses were conducted to assess the robustness of the pooled results. Some minor discrepancies were noted, such as unclear descriptions of a few screening criteria that were reportedly violated, however, it is not clear how and if they were violated at all. Additionally, there was some confusion regarding the discrepancy between the 12 studies included in the systematic review and the final 10 studies used in the meta-analysis.

 

 

Conclusions

The results show that smartphone apps can moderately reduce depressive symptoms when used as the primary therapeutic component. Given the various barriers when seeking traditional care, digital interventions have the potential to offer therapy that is available on demand, location independent and cost-effective. This potential is especially relevant for patients in remote or lower-income areas.

However, based on these results, it is not possible to recommend individual apps, since the evidence for that is limited in both quality and quantity. Future research should further investigate the impact of digital interventions on depressive symptoms and thoroughly test the safety and effectiveness of both current and future apps with clinical trials. Future app development should focus on personalization within apps or features for specific patient groups. Additionally, there should be a collaboration between developers, health care professionals and policymakers in order to attempt the integration of digital interventions in existing health systems. With enough evidence, smartphone apps could become a valuable complement to traditional therapy and contribute to a healthier population.

 

 

Study examples

Dror - Smartphone Cognitive Behavioral Therapy as an Adjunct to Pharmacotherapy for Refractory Depression: Randomized Controlled Trial (Mantani et al., 2017)

The study by Mantani et al. (2017) is an example for the potential of smartphone app interventions in relieving depression symptoms in clinically diagnosed patients with major depression disorder. The participants in the intervention group gained access to the Kokoro app, a CBT app that is based on a dialogue between two cartoon characters and additional practical exercises, alongside antidepressant treatment which was given to all participants. All participants were medication resistant, and had their antidepressants changed at the beginning of the study period.

The total sample included 164 participants. After the completion of the first session of the app, depression level, medication side effects and depression severity were measured using the PHQ-9, FIBSER and BDI-II questionnaires respectively. Then participants were randomly allocated to the intervention and control groups. Participants with PHQ-9 score>5 who tolerated and adhered to the medication and had no problem using the app were allocated to the per protocol group, while all others were allocated to the auxiliary group. The total sample gave an indication of the real-world population, while the per-protocol group was meant to give a more accurate picture of the app’s effectiveness. The per-protocol group included 117 participants out of 164. Follow-up measurements were taken at the 9th week (after the intervention) and the 17th week (after control participants had the chance to complete the intervention) to measure the effectiveness of the intervention compared to baseline.

For the total sample, the intervention had a moderate effect on the 9th week and intervention group scored 2.48 points below the control group on the PHQ (SMD=0.4; p<.001), and 4.1 points lower on the BDI-II (p=.002) on average. As for the per-protocol sample, the intervention group scored 1.72 below control on the PHQ-9 with a smaller effect size (SMD=0.28; p.02), 3.2 points on the BDI-II score (p=.05) and 0.75 points of the FIBSER score (p=.04). On the 17th week there were no differences between the groups on both samples, with the intervention group maintaining their gains from the 9th week (i.e. the control group benefited from the intervention too).

This study has shown the positive effects of the intervention app Kokoro on both samples, which exemplifies its ability to benefit a wide population of patients with major depressive disorder. It has also shown some relief in the burden of side-effects from medications in the per-control sample, which didn’t appear on the total sample. Additional important points from the study are the smaller effect in the per-protocol sample, which can be partly attributed to the higher burden of side-effects in the total sample, and the fact that 70-80% of participants weren’t remitted after two months. These show the importance of further research despite the overall positive results.

I find this study unique among those that were included in the meta-analysis because of the participation of a clinically diagnosed population, and the use of the app alongside medicine treatment. Additionally, this study has a rather high compliance rate in contrast to the low compliance of the overall sample in the meta-analysis, which allows a better understanding of the effects of the intervention, while also balancing the effects of dropout via an intention-to-treat protocol. Overall, I find the transparent and specific methodology used in this study positive, and I believe it will allow researchers to replicate and develop it more successfully to shed more light on the effects of mHealth apps on the MDD patients population. I also find the finding regarding the effects on subjective perception of side-effects (FIBSER) very interesting and potentially useful for psychiatrists in the future after further research of the matter is done.
